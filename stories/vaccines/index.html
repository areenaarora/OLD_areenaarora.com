Overwhelmed with information overload? Here's a simplified data dive into COVID vaccines!

Over two years ago, our lives came to a standstill abruptly, unleashing a kind of chaos never seen before. The word "unprecedented" was used at an unprecented rate. I've been curious about COVID vaccines' development and wanted to do a mini-data dive.

But first, a quick recap!

December 31, 2019

The WHO China office is informed of a number cases of pneumonia of unknown etiology (unknown cause) detected in Wuhan.

March 11, 2020

WHO declares COVID-19 a pandemic.

March 17, 2020

First human trial of a COVID vaccine begins, done for Moderna's vaccine

April 30, 2020

President Trump launches Operation Warp Speed, an initiavtive to produce a COVID vaccine.

May 28, 2020

United States' COVID-19 death toll surpasses 100,000.

December 11, 2020

FDA issues an Emergency Use Authorization (EUA) for the first COVID-19 vaccine– the Pfizer-BioNTech COVID-19 vaccine.

December 18, 2020

The U.S. FDA issues an Emergency Use Authorization for the second COVID-19 vaccine – the Moderna COVID-19 vaccine.

January 26, 2021

Worldwide COVID-19 cases surpass 100 million.

February 27, 2021

FDA approves emergency use authorization for Johnson and Johnson's one shot COVID-19 vaccine.

April 21, 2021

U.S. surpasses 200 million vaccinations administered.

November 29, 2021

CDC recommends everyone over 18 years old who received a Pfizer or Moderna vaccine receive a booster shot six months after they are fully vaccinated.

On CDC’s website, the words explaining vaccine ingredients for both Pfizer and Moderna start with the same two sentences:

All COVID-19 vaccine ingredients are safe. Nearly all the ingredients in COVID-19 vaccines are ingredients found in many foods—fats, sugars, and salts. The COVID-19 vaccine also contains a harmless piece of messenger RNA (mRNA).

This, and the fact that different pharma companies’ vaccines showed different efficiency ratings in testing, got me thinking how different are they anyway? Since they’re all meant to fight COVID-19, aren’t they bound to be fairly similar in composition? I’m no biochemist, of course, but I wanted an easily digestible, data-backed explanation for myself. So, here we are!

Of its eight ingredients, Pfizer has four in common with Moderna's six. Both these vaccines are mRNA type, meaning they are vaccines that teach our cells how to make a protein that will trigger an immune response. Johnson and Johnson vaccine, on the other hand, works by introducing a piece of modified virus to help the body generate an immune response.
